Detailed Course Notes

1. What is Competitive Advantage?

[cite_start]

A **competitive advantage** is a significant and (ideally) long-term benefit to a company over its competition[cite: 2740].

This advantage can result in:

    [cite_start]
  • Higher-quality products[cite: 2741].
    • [cite_start]
  • Better customer service[cite: 2742].
    • [cite_start]
  • Lower costs[cite: 2743].

An organization's goals are determined by its **competitive strategy**. [cite_start]This strategy, in turn, determines every information system's structure, features, and functions [cite: 2748-2753].

2. Porter's Five Forces Model

[cite_start]

This model, created by Michael Porter, is used to analyze the competitive forces within an industry[cite: 2758].

  • 1. [cite_start]Rivalry Among Existing Competitors: This force is high when there are many competitors, low product differentiation, and high fixed costs to enter or leave[cite: 2760, 2776].
  • 2. [cite_start]Threat of New Entrants: This threat is high when entry and exit costs are low, and the necessary technology is commonly available (e.g., opening a small restaurant) [cite: 2761, 2783-2784].
  • 3. [cite_start]Threat of Substitute Products/Services: This is high when consumers can obtain similar products that satisfy their needs from other companies (e.g., digital cameras substituting for film cameras) [cite: 2762, 2791-2793].
  • 4. [cite_start]Bargaining Power of Buyers (Customers): This power is high when customers have many choices and can easily threaten to switch to rivals[cite: 2763, 2800].
  • 5. [cite_start]Bargaining Power of Suppliers: This power is high when suppliers are strong, few in number, or provide a critical component, giving them more influence[cite: 2763, 2802].

3. Porter's Three Generic Strategies

[cite_start]

These are three approaches to beat the competition within an industry[cite: 2811]:

  1. Overall Cost Leadership: The strategy is to deliver the lowest possible cost for products and services. [cite_start]This is often achieved through aggressive negotiations with suppliers and increasing efficiency [cite: 2812, 2819-2821].
    2. [cite_start]
  2. Differentiation: The strategy is to deliver different products and services, giving customers more choices or higher quality (e.g., car companies making many different models from the same basic parts) [cite: 2814, 2824-2825].
    3. [cite_start]
  3. Focus (Niche Strategy): This strategy concentrates on offering products to a particular market segment, buyer group, or geographic market (e.g., Porsche making only high-performance sports cars, Rolex making only high-quality expensive watches) [cite: 2815, 2831, 2834-2836].

4. The Value Chain

A **Value Chain** is a network of value-creating activities. [cite_start]**Value** is the amount a customer is willing to spend, and **Margin** is the difference between that value and the cost of the activity [cite: 2842-2845]. It is split into two types of activities:

A. Primary Activities

[cite_start]

Activities that directly contribute to the creation and delivery of the product [cite: 2860, 2871-2875]:

  • Inbound Logistics: Receiving, warehousing, and inventory control of input materials.
  • Operations: Transforming inputs into the final product.
  • Outbound Logistics: Getting the finished product to the customer (warehousing, order fulfillment).
  • Marketing and Sales: Activities to get buyers to purchase (e.g., advertising, pricing).
  • Service: Activities that maintain the product's value (e.g., customer support, repair).

B. Support Activities

[cite_start]

Activities that "support" the primary activities [cite: 2851, 2849-2853]:

  • Firm Infrastructure: General management, finance, accounting.
  • Human Resources: Recruiting, training, and managing people.
  • Technology Development: R&D and IT systems.
  • Procurement: The function of purchasing the inputs.

5. E-Commerce and E-Government

[cite_start]

E-Commerce Types [cite: 2921-2924]

  • B2B (Business-to-Business): All participants are organizations (e.g., a car manufacturer buying tires from a supplier).
  • B2C (Business-to-Consumer): Organizations sell directly to individual customers (e.g., Amazon, Netflix).
  • C2C (Consumer-to-Consumer): Consumers sell directly to other consumers (e.g., OLX, eBay).
[cite_start]

E-Government Types [cite: 2928-2931]

The use of IT to simplify government services.

  • G2C (Government-to-Consumer): Citizens submit tax returns online or renew auto licenses.
  • G2B (Government-to-Business): Firms bid on government contracts online.
  • G2G (Government-to-Government): Improves communications between various levels of government (e.g., federal and state agencies sharing data).
[cite_start]

Three Key E-Commerce Challenges [cite: 2937-2940]

  1. Defining an effective e-commerce model and strategy.
  2. Dealing with consumer privacy concerns.
  3. Overcoming consumers' lack of trust.

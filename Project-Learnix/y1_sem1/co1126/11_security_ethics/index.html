Detailed Course Notes

1. Ethics

Ethics are the principles and standards that guide our behavior toward other people. Actions in ethical dilemmas are determined by your basic ethical structure and the circumstances of the situation.

  • Basic Ethical Structure: What you personally consider to be minor, serious, or very serious violations.
  • Circumstances: Include consequences, society's opinion, likelihood of effect, time to consequences, and relatedness to the people affected.

Intellectual Property

  • Intellectual Property: Intangible creative work that is embodied in physical form.
  • Copyright: Legal protection afforded an expression of an idea (e.g., a song, a book, software).
  • Fair Use Doctrine: Allows you to use copyrighted material in certain situations (e.g., for teaching, news reporting).
  • Pirated Software: The unauthorized use, duplication, distribution, or sale of copyrighted software.

2. Privacy

Privacy is the right to be left alone when you want to be, to have control over your own personal possessions, and not to be observed without your consent.

Privacy Threats from Individuals

  • Key Logger (Key Trapper): Software that, when installed, records every keystroke and mouse click.
  • Hardware Key Logger: A hardware device that captures keystrokes moving between the keyboard and the motherboard.
  • Event Data Recorders (EDR): Located in a car's airbag control module, it collects data from your car as you are driving.
  • Identity Theft: The forging of someone's identity for the purpose of fraud.
  • Phishing (Carding/Brand Spoofing): A technique to gain personal information for identity theft, often via deceptive emails.
  • Pharming: Rerouting your request for a legitimate Web site to a fake, malicious site.

Privacy and Employees

  • As of March 2005, 60% of employers monitored employee e-mails.
  • Cyberslacking: The misuse of company resources, such as visiting inappropriate sites, gaming, or chatting during work hours.
  • Reasons for Monitoring: To ensure appropriate behavior, avoid litigation for employee misconduct, and hire the best people.

Privacy and Consumers (Web)

Threat Definition
Cookie A small file that a Web site places on your computer, containing information about you and your Web activities.
Spam Unsolicited e-mail from businesses advertising goods and services.
Adware Software to generate ads that installs itself when you download another program.
Spyware Software that comes hidden in downloaded software and helps itself to your computer resources.
Trojan Horse Software you don't want hidden inside software you *do* want.
Web Log One line of information for every visitor to a Web site.
Clickstream Records information about you during a Web surfing session (what sites you visited, how long, what ads you looked at).

Privacy and Government Agencies

Government agencies store vast amounts of information on people (e.g., IRS, Census Bureau, DMV).

Electronic Surveillance Tools:

  • Carnivore (DCS-1000): An FBI tool to monitor e-mail.
  • Magic Lantern: A software key logger used by the FBI.
  • Echelon: A global NSA (National Security Agency) system that collects electronic information by satellite.

Key Laws:

  • HIPAA: Protects personal health information.
  • Financial Services Modernization Act: Requires financial institutions to protect personal customer information.

3. Security

Attacks can come from inside or outside a company. **Employee misconduct is generally more costly** than assaults from outside.

Outside Threats

  • Hackers: Knowledgeable computer users who use their knowledge to invade other people's computers.
  • Computer Virus: Software written with malicious intent to cause annoyance or damage.
  • Worm: A type of virus that spreads itself from computer to computer, usually via e-mail.
  • Denial-of-Service (DoS) Attack: Floods a Web site with so many requests for service that it slows down or crashes.

Security Measures

Measure Function
Anti-virus Detects and removes or quarantines computer viruses.
Firewall Hardware and/or software that protects a computer or network from intruders.
Encryption Scrambles the contents of a file so you can't read it without the decryption key.
Public Key Encryption (PKE) An encryption system with two keys: a public key for everyone and a private key for only the recipient.
Biometrics The use of physiological characteristics (e.g., fingerprint, retina) for identification.

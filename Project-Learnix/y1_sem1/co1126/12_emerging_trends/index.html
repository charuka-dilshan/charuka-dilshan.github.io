Detailed Course Notes

1. The Need for Information Filtering

We are moving from a "pull" to a "push" model for information.

  • Pull Technology: You request the information you want (e.g., visiting a website).
  • Push Technology: Information is sent to you based on what you *might* want (e.g., subscription notifications).
  • Information Supplier Convergence: Receiving multiple types of information (like your newspaper and internet access) from a single company.

2. The Movement Toward Intellectual Computing

This trend focuses on creating more intelligent systems that can understand and interact with us naturally.

  • Automatic Speech Understanding: The next level beyond simple speech recognition. The goal is for software to understand the *meaning* and *context* of what is said, not just transcribe the words.
  • Biometrics: The use of physical characteristics for identification (e.g., fingerprint, retina scan, voice sound).
  • Virtual Reality (VR): A three-dimensional computer simulation in which you actively and physically participate.
  • CAVE (Cave Automatic Virtual Environment): A special 3-D virtual reality room that can display images of other people and objects from other CAVEs, allowing for immersive global collaboration.
  • Holographic Devices: Devices that create, capture, or display images in true three-dimensional form.

3. Increasing Portability and Mobility

  • Portability: How easy it is to carry your technology.
  • Mobility: What you can do with your technology while carrying it.

Key Mobile Technologies:

Technology Description
Internet Phone Calls (VoIP) Using the internet to make phone calls, often for free or at a low cost.
Micro-payments Techniques to facilitate the exchange of small amounts of money for an Internet transaction.
Financial Cybermediaries Internet-based companies that make it easy for one person to pay another (e.g., PayPal).
Wearable Computers A fully-equipped computer you wear as clothing or an accessory.
Implant Chips A technology-enabled microchip implanted into the human body, often containing memory for information or GPS for location.
GPS (Global Positioning System) A collection of 24 earth-orbiting satellites that transmit radio signals to determine your longitude, latitude, speed, and direction.

4. The Digital Frontier

This trend explores new ways to interact with digital information.

  • Digital Economy: An economy marked by the electronic movement of all types of information, including physiological info (biometrics) and 3-D holograms.
  • Last-mile Bottleneck: The problem where information travels over a very fast line (Internet backbone) but then must travel over a slower line to reach your home.
  • Three-Dimensional (3-D) Technology: Presentations that give the illusion that an object is in the room with you.
  • Digital Cash (e-cash): An electronic representation of cash.

5. The Rebirth of E-Commerce

E-commerce is expanding beyond B2B and B2C into new areas.

Explosion of C2C E-Commerce

Consumer-to-Consumer (C2C) e-commerce is expected to grow dramatically (e.g., online auctions, classifieds).

Broadening of E-Government

E-Government involves using IT to perform government activities and services.

Type Description
G2G (Government-to-Government) Activities within a single nation's government.
G2B (Government-to-Business) Activities between a government and its business partners.
G2C (Government-to-Consumer) Activities between a government and its citizens.
IG2G (International G2G) Activities between two or more governments.

6. Most Important Considerations

  • Closing the Great Digital Divide: Ensuring the power of technology is realized on a worldwide scale, not just in wealthy nations.
  • Exchanging Privacy for Convenience: Considering how much of your personal privacy you are giving up in exchange for convenient new technologies.
  • Ethics: Using technology for the betterment of people and society, not just for profit.

Detailed Course Notes

1. The Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC)

The **SDLC (Systems Development Life Cycle)** is a structured, step-by-step approach for developing information systems. It is one approach used to reduce the high risk of failure in IT projects.

The Seven Phases of the SDLC

Phase Primary Activities
1. Plan Define the system, set the **project scope**, and develop the **project plan** (tasks, resources, timeframes). This phase identifies **Critical Success Factors (CSFs)**.
2. Analysis Gather the **business requirements** for the system. This is often done using **JAD (Joint Application Development)** sessions, resulting in a **Requirements Definition Document** that gets a formal **sign-off**.
3. Design Design the **technical architecture** (hardware, software, telecom). This phase involves **modeling**, such as creating **GUI (Graphical User Interface)** designs.
4. Develop Transform the design documents into an actual system. This includes building the technical architecture, creating the database, and writing the programs.
5. Test Verify that the system works and meets all business requirements. This involves executing **Test Conditions** and performing **UAT (User Acceptance Testing)** to see if the system satisfies users.
6. Implement Distribute the system to users. This phase includes creating **user documentation** and conducting user **training** (either online or in a workshop).
7. Maintain Monitor and support the new system to ensure it continues to meet business goals. This includes building a **Help Desk** and having a process for system changes.

2. Why Do Systems Fail?

Only about 20% of IT projects are successful. The primary reasons for failure include:

  • Unclear or Missing Requirements: The business requirements drive the entire project. If they are wrong, the system will fail.
  • Skipping SDLC Phases: When a project falls behind, teams tend to skip phases, which is "sure to lead to system failure".
  • Failure to Manage Project Scope:
    • Scope Creep: Occurs when the scope of the project increases.
    • Feature Creep: Occurs when developers add extra features that were not part of the initial requirements.
  • Failure to Manage Project Plan: The plan is the road map. Failing to manage, revise, and update it is a major challenge.
  • Changing Technology: Technology changes so fast it can make the system obsolete before it's even finished.

3. Sourcing: Who Builds the System?

Sourcing is the question of who will build the system.

A. Insourcing

Using IT specialists **within your organization** to develop the system.

B. Selfsourcing (or End-User Development)

The development of IT systems by **Knowledge Workers (KWs)** with little or no help from IT specialists.

  • Advantages: Improves requirements determination, increases user participation and sense of ownership, and increases the speed of development.
  • Risks: Inadequate user expertise, lack of organizational focus ("privatized" systems), and lack of documentation or support.

C. Outsourcing

The delegation of specific work to a **third party** for a specified time, cost, and level of service.

  • Advantages: Allows focus on core competencies, reduces costs, acquires leading-edge technology, and improves performance accountability.
  • Disadvantages: Reduces technical know-how, reduces degree of control, increases vulnerability of strategic information, and increases dependency on others.

4. Prototyping

Prototyping is an alternative to the traditional SDLC.

  • Prototype: A model that demonstrates the features of a proposed product, service, or system.
  • Prototyping: The process of building that model.

Types of Prototypes:

  • Proof-of-Concept Prototype: Used to prove the technical feasibility of a proposed system.
  • Selling Prototype: Used to convince people of the worth of a proposed system.

Advantages of Prototyping:

  • Encourages active knowledge worker participation.
  • Helps resolve discrepancies among knowledge workers.
  • Gives users a "feel" for the final system.

Disadvantages of Prototyping:

  • Leads people to believe the final system will follow shortly.
  • Gives no indication of performance under operational conditions.
  • Leads the team to forgo proper testing and documentation.

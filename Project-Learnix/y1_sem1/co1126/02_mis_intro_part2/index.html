Detailed Course Notes

1. The Importance of MIS

In today's complex business world, a properly planned, analyzed, designed, and maintained MIS is critical for survival and growth. [cite_start]It provides **timely, reliable, and useful information** to help management make speedy and rational decisions[cite: 3].

Two Main Challenges for Modern Managers:

  1. Liberalization and Globalization: Organizations must compete globally, not just locally. A manager must make quick decisions, otherwise business will be taken by competitors. [cite_start]This enhances the necessity for an MIS [cite: 5-6].
  2. The Information Age: Information doubles every two to three years. [cite_start]A manager must process large, voluminous data; failing to do so can lead to wrong decisions that are costly to the company [cite: 7-8].
[cite_start]

MIS is considered the **"nerve center"** of an organization[cite: 10]. It assists decision-makers at various stages to help achieve predetermined goals.

[cite_start]

Warning: A poorly planned or maintained MIS can provide inaccurate, irrelevant, or obsolete information, which may prove **fatal** for the organization [cite: 12-13].

The Competitive Edge

[cite_start]

MIS enables even small organizations to more than offset the **economies of scale** enjoyed by their bigger competitors, providing a competitive edge[cite: 14].

2. Computers and MIS

Computers and information technology are used to automate the critical functions of an MIS. [cite_start]Owners and managers rely on computers to access information from the system [cite: 20-23].

    [cite_start]
  • Processing Speed & Accuracy: Computers can process certain types of business data (like accounting and finance) **quicker and more accurately** than employees can [cite: 24-25].
  • Access Control & Security: Using computers allows a company to restrict employee access to sensitive business or financial information. [cite_start]Most systems require **usernames and passwords**, which prevents individuals from filtering or altering information before managers review it [cite: 27-28].

3. Organizational Structure

MIS supports all functional areas and levels of an organization. [cite_start]A typical structure includes [cite: 33-49]:

  • Top Level: President
  • Management Level: R&D Manager, Finance Manager, Marketing Manager, Manufacturing Manager.
  • Operational Level:
    • R&D: Design, Development, Testing
    • Finance: Accounting, Purchasing
    • Marketing: Sales, Customer Service
    • Manufacturing: Line Supervisors, Production Teams, Distribution

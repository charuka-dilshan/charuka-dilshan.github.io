Detailed Course Notes

1. Four Phases of Decision Making

The process of making a decision can be broken down into four distinct phases:

  1. Intelligence (Find what to fix): You find or recognize a problem, need, or opportunity. [cite_start]This is also called the diagnostic phase of decision making [cite: 1383-1384].
  2. Design (Find fixes): You consider possible ways of solving the problem, filling the need, or taking advantage of the opportunity. [cite_start]In this phase, you develop all the possible solutions you can [cite: 1385-1387].
    3. [cite_start]
  3. Choice (Pick a fix): You examine and weigh the merits of each solution, estimate the consequences of each, and choose the best one [cite: 1388-1389].
    4. [cite_start]
  4. Implementation (Apply the fix): You carry out the chosen solution, monitor the results, and make adjustments as necessary [cite: 1390-1391].

2. Types of Decisions You Face

Decisions can be categorized based on their structure and frequency:

  • Structured Decision: A decision that involves processing a certain kind of information in a specified way so that you will always get the right answer. [cite_start]No "feel" or intuition is necessary [cite: 1396-1398].
  • Unstructured Decision: A decision for which there may be several "right" answers, and there is no precise way to get a single right answer. [cite_start]This often involves intuition and judgment [cite: 1399-1400].
    • [cite_start]
  • Recurring Decision: A decision that happens repeatedly, and often periodically (e.g., weekly, monthly, quarterly) [cite: 1401-1402].
  • Nonrecurring (Ad Hoc) Decision: A decision that you make infrequently, perhaps only once. [cite_start]The criteria for the best solution may be different each time [cite: 1403-1404].

3. Decision Support Systems (DSS)

[cite_start]

A **Decision Support System (DSS)** is a highly flexible and interactive IT system that is designed to support decision-making when the problem is **not structured**[cite: 1408].

The DSS Alliance

[cite_start]

A DSS is an alliance between you (the decision maker) and the IT system[cite: 1409].

[cite_start] [cite_start]
What You Bring What IT Brings
Experience, Intuition, Judgment, Knowledge [cite: 1411]Speed, Vast Information, Processing Capabilities [cite: 1410]
[cite_start]

The primary objective of a DSS is to improve your **effectiveness** as a decision maker, not just your efficiency[cite: 1416].

[cite_start]

Components of a DSS [cite: 1428-1433]

    [cite_start]
  1. Model Management Component: Stores and maintains the DSS's models (e.g., What-If Models, Optimization Models, Goal-Seeking Models)[cite: 1453, 1455, 1457].
    2. [cite_start]
  2. Data Management Component: Stores and maintains the information that you want your DSS to use (e.g., Organizational Information, External Information, Personal Information)[cite: 1456, 1458].
  3. User Interface Management Component: Allows you to communicate with the DSS. It consists of the user interface and the system that manages it.

4. Geographic Information Systems (GIS)

[cite_start]

A **Geographic Information System (GIS)** is a decision support system designed specifically to work with **spatial information**[cite: 1485].

[cite_start]

Spatial information is any information that can be shown in map form, such as roads, property values, or population distributions[cite: 1486].

[cite_start]

Businesses use GIS to [cite: 1558-1562]:

  • Identify the best site to locate a new branch office.
  • Target pockets of potential customers.
  • Reposition promotions and advertising.
  • Determine the optimal location for a new distribution outlet.

5. Artificial Intelligence (AI)

[cite_start]

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is the science of making machines imitate human thinking and behavior[cite: 1566].

[cite_start]

A **Robot** is a mechanical device equipped with simulated human senses and the capability of taking action on its own[cite: 1567].

[cite_start]

Major Categories of AI in Business [cite: 1587-1591]

1. Expert Systems (or Knowledge-Based Systems)
[cite_start]

An AI system that applies reasoning capabilities to reach a conclusion[cite: 1595]. [cite_start]They are built for specific application areas called **domains** (e.g., medicine, finance) [cite: 1604-1611].

  • Diagnostic Problems: Solves "what's wrong?" (corresponds to the Intelligence Phase) [cite_start][cite: 1597-1598].
  • Prescriptive Problems: Solves "what to do?" (corresponds to the Choice Phase) [cite_start][cite: 1599-1600].
[cite_start]

Expert systems **cannot** use common sense or automate all processes [cite: 1625-1627].

2. Neural Networks (or Artificial Neural Network, ANN)

An AI system that is capable of finding and differentiating patterns. [cite_start]It simulates the human ability to classify things and can **learn by example** [cite: 1631-1634].

[cite_start]

Uses in business include fraud detection, real estate appraisal, and target marketing [cite: 1647-1652].

3. Genetic Algorithms
[cite_start]

An AI system that mimics the evolutionary, **"survival-of-the-fittest" process** to generate increasingly better solutions to a problem[cite: 1666].

[cite_start]

Genetic algorithms are **optimizing systems**; they find the combination of inputs that give the best outputs[cite: 1668].

[cite_start]

Used to find optimal project combinations or determine the best network configuration[cite: 1673, 1676].

4. Intelligent Agents
[cite_start]

Software that assists you, or acts on your behalf, in performing repetitive computer-related tasks[cite: 1680].

[cite_start]

Key components are: **Autonomy** (acts on its own), **Adaptively** (learns), and **Sociability** (confers with other agents) [cite: 1700-1702].
